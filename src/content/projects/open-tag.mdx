## About the Project

OpenTag started as a summer holiday project I wanted to build together with my kids. Instead of buying an expensive commercial laser tag set, we decided it would be much more fun to design and build our own from scratch.

What started as a simple family project quickly turned into something much bigger. Every challenge led to another idea, another feature, another opportunity to learn. Along the way we've been picking up electronics, PCB design, embedded programming, ESP32 development, 3D printing, optics, game design, and Android development.

The goal isn't to compete with commercial laser tag systems. The goal is to create something that anyone can build, repair, customize, and improve. If this project inspires other families to spend a few weekends building something together instead of simply buying it, it's already achieved one of its biggest goals.

## Design Philosophy

OpenTag follows a few simple principles:

- Completely open source
- Built using widely available components
- Printable on consumer 3D printers
- Easy to repair
- Modular
- Easy to modify
- Designed for learning
- Community driven
- Long-term maintainable

## Open Hardware

OpenTag is intentionally designed using off-the-shelf parts. Builders can source components from suppliers like DigiKey, Mouser, Amazon, AliExpress, or local electronics stores.

The PCB is designed around commonly available modules: the XIAO ESP32-S3, a MAX98357A audio amplifier, a DRV8833 motor driver, an SSD1306 OLED, and WS2812 LEDs. If a module breaks, it can simply be replaced — no proprietary electronics.

### Schematic

<div class="not-prose my-6">
  <Image
    src={schematic}
    alt="OpenTag blaster wiring schematic"
    class="w-full rounded-[var(--radius-xl)] border border-[var(--border)] object-cover"
  />
</div>

The XIAO ESP32-S3 sits at the center and fans its GPIOs out to every subsystem: the IR emitter
circuit (top right — a small resistor/capacitor network driving the IR LED for hit detection),
the haptic feedback motor (through its driver board), the MAX98357A amp driving a small speaker,
a physical button, and two "Frame" headers that break out to the addressable LED strips running
along the blaster's frame. A power switch and a 470nF capacitor sit on the main VCC/GND rail for
basic power filtering, with everything running off a single 3.7V 1800mAh 18650 Li-ion cell.
